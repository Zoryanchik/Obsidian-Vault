### Hard Paywall

- **How it works:** Users must pay the full price or commit to a subscription before they can access any part of the software. There is no free preview.
    
- **Why it's used:** This is often the default for early-stage products because it requires the least engineering effort to build (no need to manage trial expirations or feature-gating). It guarantees that every active user is generating revenue, but it creates the highest friction for new customer acquisition.
    

### Paid Trial

- **How it works:** Users pay a small, nominal fee (e.g., $1 or $5) to access the product for a strictly limited time.
    
- **Why it's used:** This model lowers the initial barrier to entry compared to a hard paywall but acts as a strong filter for intent. By requiring a credit card upfront, it weeds out casual browsers and spam accounts, ensuring that only users with some genuine willingness to pay enter the funnel.
    

### Free Trial

- **How it works:** Users are given full or partial access to the product for free, but only for a limited window of time (commonly 7, 14, or 30 days). Once the time is up, they hit a paywall.
    
- **Why it's used:** As a product matures, companies use free trials to widen the top of their acquisition funnel. It allows users to experience the product's value firsthand before making a financial commitment. This model requires a smooth onboarding experience so users can find value before the clock runs out.
    

### Freemium

- **How it works:** A basic version of the product is available for free forever. Users are only prompted to pay if they want to unlock advanced "premium" features, remove branding, or increase their usage limits (e.g., more storage or extra seats).
    
- **Why it's used:** This model requires the highest level of product maturity. It casts the widest possible net for user acquisition and relies heavily on product-led growth. To work successfully, the cost of serving a free user must be very low, and the product must be intuitive enough that a small percentage of users naturally upgrade to the paid tiers without needing intervention from a sales team.
